# Commonly Missed Deductions and Credits

Organized by likelihood of being missed × dollar impact.

## Tier 1: High Impact, Frequently Missed

### 1. Incorrect RSU/ESPP Cost Basis ($$$$)
**What**: Brokerages report RSU sales on 1099-B with cost basis = $0 or the wrong amount
**Why missed**: Employer reports RSU income on W-2 but the brokerage doesn't adjust cost basis
**Impact**: You're taxed TWICE — once as income (W-2) and once as capital gain (wrong basis)
**Fix**: Manually enter correct cost basis = FMV on vesting date × shares
**Estimated savings**: Can be thousands to tens of thousands

### 2. HSA Contribution Deduction ($1,000-$3,000)
**What**: HSA contributions made outside of payroll are deductible on Line 13 of Schedule 1
**Why missed**: People who contribute to HSA via bank transfer (not payroll) forget to deduct
**Impact**: $4,300 (individual) or $8,550 (family) × marginal rate
**Fix**: Ensure Form 8889 is filed, deduction taken on Schedule 1

### 3. Foreign Tax Credit on International Investments ($100-$1,000)
**What**: Tax paid to foreign governments on international stock dividends
**Why missed**: People see a small "foreign tax paid" on 1099-DIV and ignore it
**Impact**: Direct tax credit (not just deduction) — dollar-for-dollar
**Fix**: File Form 1116 or claim on Form 1040 Line 1 (if under $300/$600)

### 4. Student Loan Interest Deduction ($0-$550)
**What**: Up to $2,500 in student loan interest is deductible (above-the-line)
**Why missed**: Income phaseout confusion; people assume they don't qualify
**Impact**: $2,500 × marginal rate = up to $925 at 37% bracket
**Phaseout**: Starts at $80,000 single / $165,000 MFJ MAGI

### 5. Saver's Credit ($200-$2,000)
**What**: Tax CREDIT (not deduction) for retirement contributions
**Why missed**: People don't realize it exists; income limits are generous
**Impact**: 10-50% of first $2,000 contributed ($4,000 MFJ) = up to $1,000/$2,000 credit
**Income limit**: AGI < $39,500 single / $79,000 MFJ (2025)

### 6. Charitable Contribution of Appreciated Stock
**What**: Donating stock directly instead of selling, paying tax, then donating cash
**Why missed**: People default to cash donations without considering stock
**Impact**: Avoid capital gains tax on appreciation + get full FMV deduction
**Example**: Stock worth $10,000, basis $3,000. Donate directly: save $7,000 × 15-20% = $1,050-$1,400 in capital gains tax PLUS get $10,000 deduction

### 7. Electric Vehicle Credit ($3,750-$7,500)
**What**: Tax credit for new EVs ($7,500 max) or used EVs ($4,000 max)
**Why missed**: Complex eligibility rules; many assume they don't qualify
**Requirements (new)**: Vehicle MSRP caps, assembly location, battery sourcing requirements
**Requirements (used)**: AGI < $75k single / $150k MFJ; vehicle price < $25k

### 8. Residential Clean Energy Credit (30%)
**What**: 30% credit on solar panels, battery storage, heat pumps, etc.
**Why missed**: People don't realize battery storage and heat pumps qualify
**Impact**: 30% of cost, no cap. $30,000 solar system = $9,000 credit

## Tier 2: Medium Impact, Commonly Overlooked

### 9. Medical Expense Deduction (if >7.5% of AGI)
**What**: Medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI are deductible (if itemizing)
**Why missed**: People don't realize the threshold is 7.5%, or don't total up expenses
**Qualifying expenses**: premiums (not employer-paid), dental, vision, prescriptions, mental health, fertility treatments, weight loss programs (if prescribed), medical travel

### 10. State Tax Refund from Prior Year
**What**: If you itemized AND received a state tax refund, the refund is taxable income
**Why missed**: Not actually "missed" — this is the reverse. People sometimes fail to report it.
**Note**: Only taxable if you itemized and benefited from the SALT deduction in the prior year

### 11. Capital Loss Carryforward
**What**: Unused capital losses from previous years carry forward indefinitely
**Why missed**: People forget to apply prior-year losses or don't realize they carry forward
**Impact**: Offsets capital gains dollar-for-dollar, plus $3,000/year against ordinary income
**Fix**: Check prior year Schedule D for carryforward amounts

### 12. Home Office Deduction (Self-Employed Only)
**What**: Deduction for dedicated home office space used for self-employment
**Why missed**: Fear of audit; confusion about who qualifies (W-2 employees do NOT qualify)
**Impact**: Simplified method: $5/sq ft, max 300 sq ft = $1,500 deduction
**Regular method**: actual expenses × business-use percentage (can be much larger)

### 13. Educator Expense Deduction ($300)
**What**: K-12 teachers can deduct $300 in classroom supplies (above-the-line)
**Why missed**: Small amount, people forget
**Impact**: $300 × marginal rate = $66-$111

### 14. Self-Employment Tax Deduction (50%)
**What**: 50% of self-employment tax is deductible (above-the-line)
**Why missed**: Some tax software handles this automatically, but manual filers miss it
**Impact**: If SE tax = $10,000, deduction = $5,000 × marginal rate

### 15. Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction — Section 199A
**What**: 20% deduction on qualified business income from pass-through entities
**Why missed**: Complex rules, income limitations, specified service trade restrictions
**Impact**: 20% of QBI = significant for self-employed and business owners
**Phaseout**: Starts at $191,950 single / $383,900 MFJ (2025)

## Tier 3: Situational but Valuable

### 16. Adoption Credit ($17,280)
**What**: Nonrefundable credit for qualified adoption expenses
**Why missed**: People don't realize the full scope of qualifying expenses

### 17. Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit
**What**: 30% credit on qualifying energy improvements (insulation, windows, doors, HVAC)
**Annual cap**: $1,200 general / $2,000 for heat pumps

### 18. Child and Dependent Care Credit
**What**: Credit for childcare expenses so you can work
**Why missed**: People use Dependent Care FSA and assume they can't also claim the credit
**Note**: You can use BOTH if expenses exceed FSA limit, but coordinate carefully

### 19. American Opportunity Tax Credit ($2,500/student)
**What**: 40% refundable credit for college expenses
**Why missed**: Income phaseout confusion; not realizing it covers required course materials
**Phaseout**: $80,000-$90,000 single / $160,000-$180,000 MFJ

### 20. Gambling Loss Deduction
**What**: Gambling losses are deductible up to the amount of gambling winnings
**Why missed**: People report winnings but forget they can offset with losses
**Requirement**: Must itemize; need adequate records of losses

## Red Flags to Check

### Signs of Overpayment
- Large refund year after year (you're giving the IRS an interest-free loan)
- Filing single when Head of Household is available
- Standard deduction when itemized would be higher (or vice versa)
- No HSA contributions despite having HDHP
- No retirement contributions (missing both deduction AND future tax-free growth)
- Not using tax-advantaged accounts for investments

### Signs of Potential Errors
- RSU/ESPP cost basis showing $0 on 1099-B
- No foreign tax credit despite holding international funds
- No capital loss carryforward despite previous years' losses
- Withholding rate drastically different from effective tax rate
- AMT being paid without corresponding ISO exercises or large SALT deductions
