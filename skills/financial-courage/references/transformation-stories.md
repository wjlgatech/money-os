## Story Bank

### Story 1: The Teacher Who Built $800K
**Profile match:** Low-to-moderate income, feels like investing "isn't for people like me"
**Before:** Sarah, 28, public school teacher making $42K. $23K in student loans. $400 in savings.
Felt like investing was "for tech bros and trust fund kids." Avoided looking at her bank account.
**Turning point:** Discovered her school's 403(b) had a 5% employer match she wasn't capturing.
That's $2,100/year in free money she'd been leaving on the table for 3 years = $6,300 gone.
**Actions taken:**
1. Enrolled in 403(b) at 5% to capture full match (Week 1)
2. Opened Roth IRA, started $100/month into total market index fund (Month 1)
3. Set up automatic $200/month extra on student loans (Month 2)
4. Moved $3K emergency fund from checking (0.01%) to HYSA (4.5%) (Month 2)
**Result after 5 years:** Student loans paid off. $47K in retirement accounts. $8K emergency fund.
On track for $800K+ by age 60 without changing her contribution rate.
**Key quote:** "I used to think I didn't make enough to invest. Turns out I didn't make enough NOT to."

### Story 2: The $87K Debt Payoff
**Profile match:** Overwhelmed by debt, feels hopeless, doesn't want to look at the numbers
**Before:** Marcus, 34, marketing manager. $87K in debt: $22K credit cards (19% APR),
$45K student loans (6.5%), $20K car loan (7.2%). Making minimums. Felt physically sick
thinking about it. Hadn't opened a credit card statement in 6 months.
**Turning point:** Finally listed everything out. The total was actually LESS than he feared
(he'd been catastrophizing it as "over $100K" in his head).
**Actions taken:**
1. Listed all debts with amounts and rates (Day 1 — took 20 minutes)
2. Called credit card company, negotiated rate down to 14% on $12K of the balance (Week 1)
3. Did 0% balance transfer on $10K of credit card debt (Week 2)
4. Redirected $300/month from unused subscriptions + dining to debt snowball (Month 1)
5. Sold the car, bought reliable used car for $8K, eliminated $20K debt + $380/month payment
6. Applied yearly bonus ($5K) to highest-rate debt
**Result after 3 years:** Completely debt-free. Started investing $1,500/month.
**Timeline:** 38 months from "I'm drowning" to debt-free.
**Key quote:** "The scariest part was opening the spreadsheet. Everything after that was just math."

### Story 3: The 45-Year-Old Fresh Start
**Profile match:** Feels "too late," age anxiety, comparing to peers
**Before:** Diana, 45, divorced. Had to split retirement accounts. Left with $62K in 401(k),
no other investments. Felt 20 years behind her peers. Kept seeing articles about millennials
who retired at 35 and wanted to scream.
**Turning point:** Did the math instead of the comparison. At 45, she had 20 years of
compounding ahead of her AND catch-up contribution eligibility at 50.
**Actions taken:**
1. Maxed 401(k) at $23,500/year ($1,958/month) — her employer matched 4% ($3,600/yr)
2. Opened Roth IRA, maxed at $7,000/year
3. At 50, added catch-up contributions: +$7,500 (401k) + $1,000 (IRA)
4. Invested 100% in low-cost index funds (total market + international)
5. Applied for and received a promotion (8% raise), routed 100% of raise to investments
**Result after 10 years (age 55):** $62K → $487K with contributions + growth
**Projected at 65:** $1.1M+ (not counting Social Security)
**Key quote:** "I stopped mourning the 20 years I 'lost' and started building with the 20 I had."

### Story 4: The High Earner with Nothing to Show
**Profile match:** Good income but no savings, lifestyle inflation, embarrassed
**Before:** Jason, 38, software engineer making $185K. $4K in savings. $12K in retirement.
No debt, but somehow every paycheck disappeared. Drove a leased BMW. Ate out 5x/week.
Had a vague sense of "I should be further along" that he medicated with more spending.
**Turning point:** Calculated his "wealth gap" — on his income, if he'd saved just 20% since
age 25, he'd have $480K. The gap wasn't about income. It was about routing.
**Actions taken:**
1. Set up "pay yourself first": 25% of paycheck auto-routed to investments before he saw it
2. Maxed 401(k) + Mega Backdoor Roth (his employer allowed it) = $69K/year tax-advantaged
3. Kept the BMW but stopped eating out more than 2x/week (saved $800/month)
4. Moved $3K/month to taxable brokerage in VTI
5. Automated everything — never had to think about it
**Result after 3 years:** $4K → $290K across all accounts
**Key quote:** "I didn't cut my lifestyle. I just stopped letting my bank account hit zero
before I invested. Automation is the cheat code."

### Story 5: The Single Parent Comeback
**Profile match:** Tight income, kids, feels selfish investing when kids need things
**Before:** Maria, 32, single mom of 2, making $55K as a nurse. $8K in credit card debt.
$0 in investments. Felt guilty even thinking about investing "when the kids need new shoes."
**Turning point:** Realized that NOT investing was actually hurting her kids' future more
than a pair of shoes ever could. $200/month started at 32 = $379K by 62. That $200/month
NOT invested = $0 for her kids to inherit.
**Actions taken:**
1. Applied for every benefit she qualified for (saved $300/month on childcare subsidy)
2. Snowballed credit card debt with $400/month (paid off in 22 months)
3. After debt, redirected $400/month: $200 Roth IRA, $100 529 per kid, $100 emergency fund
4. Set up Custodial Roth IRA for older kid who had babysitting income ($50/month)
5. Taught kids about compound interest using a "family wealth thermometer" on the fridge
**Result after 5 years:** Debt-free. $18K in Roth. $12K in 529s. $6K emergency fund.
Kids understand money better than most adults.
**Key quote:** "Investing $200 a month isn't selfish. It's the most selfless thing I can do —
it's buying my kids a mom who isn't financially terrified."

### Story 6: The Immigrant Who Built Generational Wealth
**Profile match:** Starting in a new country/situation, cultural pressure to send money home,
feels the system isn't built for them
**Before:** Priya, 29, first-generation immigrant, software QA making $75K. Sending $500/month
to family abroad. $2K in savings. Felt torn between family obligation and her own future.
**Turning point:** Realized she could do BOTH if she was strategic about it.
**Actions taken:**
1. Captured full employer 401(k) match (4% = $3,000/year) — this didn't reduce her family support
2. Opened Roth IRA, contributed $300/month (still sent $500/month home)
3. Moved to HYSA for emergency fund, earned $180/year more on same savings
4. Negotiated a $12K raise (asked for the first time ever), split it: $500 to investments, $500 to family
5. After 3 years, helped family start a small business with $5K seed capital (instead of ongoing $500/month)
**Result after 5 years:** $89K invested. Family business generating income independently.
Monthly support reduced to $200 by family's choice.
**Key quote:** "I used to think I had to choose between my family's present and my family's future.
Turns out compound interest doesn't care about the false dilemma."
