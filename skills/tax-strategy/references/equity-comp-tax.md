# Equity Compensation Tax Strategies

## RSU (Restricted Stock Units)

### How RSUs Are Taxed
- At vesting: shares are valued at Fair Market Value (FMV) → taxed as ORDINARY INCOME
- Employer withholds taxes (typically 22% federal supplemental rate — often under-withheld for high earners)
- Your cost basis = FMV on vesting date
- If you hold after vesting and sell later: gain/loss from vest-date FMV is capital gain/loss
- If held >1 year after vesting: LONG-TERM capital gain (15-20%)
- If held <1 year after vesting: SHORT-TERM capital gain (ordinary income rates)

### RSU Tax Strategies

**Strategy 1: Sell-to-Cover + Diversify**
Sell enough shares at vesting to cover taxes, then sell remaining over time to diversify.
Pro: reduces concentration risk. Con: miss upside if stock rises.

**Strategy 2: Hold for Long-Term Capital Gains**
Hold vested shares >1 year to convert any appreciation to long-term rates.
Pro: 15-20% rate vs 22-37% ordinary income. Con: concentration risk, stock could decline.

**Strategy 3: Donate Appreciated RSU Shares to DAF**
If you've held vested RSU shares that have appreciated significantly:
- Donate shares directly to a Donor Advised Fund
- Deduct full FMV as charitable contribution
- Pay ZERO capital gains tax on the appreciation
- Double benefit: deduction + tax-free appreciation

**Strategy 4: Bunch Vesting Into Low-Income Years**
If you have negotiation power over vesting schedule (rare), or are between jobs:
- Stack conversions/exercises in lower-income years
- Coordinate with other income events

### Common RSU Mistakes
- Not realizing withholding is at 22% flat when your marginal rate might be 32-37%
- Holding concentrated stock position "because it might go up" (concentration risk)
- Not tracking cost basis correctly when calculating gain/loss on sale

## ISO (Incentive Stock Options)

### How ISOs Are Taxed
- At grant: NO tax
- At exercise (buy shares): NO regular income tax, BUT the "bargain element" (FMV - strike price) IS counted for AMT purposes
- At sale: If held >2 years from grant AND >1 year from exercise: LONG-TERM capital gain on (sale price - strike price)
- Disqualifying disposition (sold too early): bargain element taxed as ordinary income

### ISO Tax Strategies

**Strategy 1: Exercise in Low-Income Years**
AMT is calculated on total income + ISO bargain element.
Exercise ISOs in years when other income is LOW to minimize AMT exposure.
Best scenarios: sabbatical year, between jobs, early retirement.

**Strategy 2: Partial Exercise to Stay Below AMT Threshold**
Calculate the maximum number of shares you can exercise without triggering AMT.
Exercise that many each year, spreading over multiple years.

**Strategy 3: Same-Day Sale (Disqualifying Disposition)**
If the AMT hit is too large, do a same-day exercise-and-sell.
The bargain element is taxed as ordinary income, but you avoid AMT and concentration risk.
Often the practical choice when the spread is very large.

**Strategy 4: Exercise-and-Hold for LTCG**
Exercise early, pay AMT, hold for qualifying disposition.
If stock rises: massive savings (LTCG rate vs ordinary income).
If stock falls: you've paid AMT on phantom income AND lost money. High risk.

### AMT Trap Warning
The AMT is the #1 risk for ISO holders. When you exercise ISOs and hold:
- You owe AMT on the bargain element even though you haven't sold
- This is a CASH tax bill on unrealized gains
- If the stock then crashes, you've paid tax on gains you never received
- The AMT credit carries forward but may take years to recover
- ALWAYS model AMT exposure before exercising ISOs

## NSO (Non-Qualified Stock Options)

### How NSOs Are Taxed
- At exercise: bargain element (FMV - strike price) taxed as ORDINARY INCOME
- Subject to income tax withholding + FICA taxes
- Cost basis = FMV at exercise
- Later sale: gain/loss from exercise-date FMV is capital gain/loss

### NSO Strategies
- Exercise in low-income years to minimize bracket impact
- Exercise-and-hold for long-term capital gains treatment on future appreciation
- Charitable donation of exercised NSO shares with unrealized appreciation

## ESPP (Employee Stock Purchase Plan)

### How ESPP Is Taxed (Qualified Plan)
- Purchase at discount (typically 15% below FMV)
- If held >2 years from offering AND >1 year from purchase: qualifying disposition
  - Discount is ordinary income, rest is LTCG
- If sold sooner: disqualifying disposition
  - Bargain element taxed as ordinary income

### ESPP Strategy
- ALWAYS participate if discount is 15% — it's essentially free money
- For qualifying disposition: hold shares 2+ years from offering date
- If stock is volatile: sell immediately (disqualifying) and take the guaranteed ~15% return
- Math: 15% guaranteed return over 6 months = 30%+ annualized. Hard to beat.

## 10b5-1 Plans for Concentrated Positions

For executives or insiders with large stock positions:
- Pre-schedule sales using a 10b5-1 plan (defense against insider trading claims)
- Systematic selling reduces concentration risk over time
- Required cooling-off period: 90 days for officers/directors, 30 days for others
- Must be adopted in good faith when not in possession of MNPI

## Multi-Year Planning Matrix

| Year Type | RSU Strategy | ISO Strategy | Portfolio Strategy |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| High income (big vest) | Sell to cover, donate to DAF | Don't exercise ISOs this year | Harvest losses to offset RSU income |
| Low income (gap year) | If holding RSU, this is a good year to sell | Exercise ISOs (lower AMT exposure) | Do Roth conversions, harvest gains at 0% |
| Normal year | Standard sell-to-cover + diversify | Partial exercise below AMT threshold | DCA into diversified positions |
| Pre-retirement | Accelerate diversification | Exercise remaining ISOs before they expire | Shift to income/dividend assets |
