## Bracket Optimization Strategies

### Strategy 1: Fill Up Lower Brackets (Roth Conversions)

If your taxable income is well below the top of your current bracket, you have "headroom" to realize additional income at the current rate.

Example: Single filer with $80,000 taxable income is in the 22% bracket.
Headroom to top of 22% bracket: $103,350 - $80,000 = $23,350
Action: Convert $23,350 from Traditional IRA to Roth IRA, paying 22% now.
If future bracket would be 32%+, this saves 10%+ on $23,350 = $2,335+.

### Strategy 2: Harvest Capital Gains at 0% Rate

If total income (including gains) stays below $48,350 (single) or $96,700 (MFJ), long-term capital gains are taxed at 0%.

Common situations: sabbatical year, early retirement, gap year between jobs.
Action: Sell appreciated positions, realize gains tax-free, immediately rebuy (no wash sale rule for gains).
This resets cost basis higher, reducing future tax liability.

### Strategy 3: Income Smoothing Across Years

If you have a year with unusually HIGH income (RSU mega-vest, bonus, stock sale):
- Defer deductions to that year (bunch charitable giving, prepay state taxes)
- Accelerate deductions into the high-income year
- Delay Roth conversions to a lower-income year

If you have a year with unusually LOW income (job change, sabbatical):
- Accelerate Roth conversions
- Harvest capital gains at lower rate
- Exercise ISOs (lower AMT exposure)

### Strategy 4: Bunching Deductions

If total itemized deductions are near the standard deduction ($15,000 single / $30,000 MFJ):
- Alternate years: bunch 2 years of charitable giving into 1 year (via DAF)
- Year 1: Itemize with bunched deductions
- Year 2: Take standard deduction
- Net benefit: deduction that would have been "wasted" (below standard) is captured

### Strategy 5: State Tax Arbitrage

If you live in a high-tax state (CA, NY, NJ) and plan to move to a no-tax state (FL, TX, WA, NV):
- Defer income recognition until after the move
- Delay RSU vesting elections or stock option exercises if possible
- Convert Traditional IRA to Roth AFTER moving to the no-income-tax state

SALT deduction cap: $10,000 (as of 2025). This limits the federal benefit of state taxes paid.
