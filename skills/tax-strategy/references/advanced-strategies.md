# Advanced Tax Strategies

Strategies that most retail investors and even many CPAs don't proactively surface.
Each strategy includes: what it is, who qualifies, estimated savings, and action steps.

## 1. Qualified Small Business Stock (Section 1202 — QSBS)

### What It Is
Exclude up to $15 million (or 10x basis) in capital gains from the sale of qualified small business stock. Potentially the most valuable tax benefit available to startup investors and founders.

### Who Qualifies
- Stock must be in a domestic C-corporation
- Corporation gross assets < $75 million at time of stock issuance (post-July 2025 rules)
- Stock must be held for 5+ years (full exclusion), 3 years (50%), or 4 years (75%)
- Must be original issuance (bought directly from the company, not secondary market)
- Corporation must be in a qualifying business (tech, manufacturing YES; professional services, finance, hospitality NO)

### Estimated Savings
- On $1M gain: up to $200,000 saved (avoiding 20% LTCG)
- On $10M gain: up to $2,000,000 saved
- With stacking (gifting shares to family): multiply exclusion per person

### Action Steps
1. Review any private company stock held — does it qualify as QSBS?
2. Start tracking holding period from acquisition date
3. Consider gifting shares to family members BEFORE sale to multiply the exclusion cap
4. Ensure the company maintains C-corp status and stays below the gross asset threshold

## 2. Qualified Opportunity Zone (QOZ) Investments

### What It Is
Invest capital gains into a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF) to defer and potentially reduce tax on those gains.

### Current Rules (2025-2026)
- Invest capital gains within 180 days of realization into a QOF
- CRITICAL DEADLINE: deferred gains must be recognized by December 31, 2026
- If held 10+ years: gains on the QOF investment itself are TAX-FREE
- The 10-year exclusion on NEW gains remains the primary benefit going forward

### Who Benefits
- Anyone with large realized capital gains looking to defer
- Real estate investors, stock sellers with concentrated gains
- Must invest in QOF within 180 days of gain realization

### Action Steps
1. If you have large gains this year, research QOF options BEFORE selling
2. Plan the 2026 recognition event — ensure cash/liquidity to pay deferred tax
3. For new investments: the 10-year exclusion on appreciation is still very valuable

## 3. Donor Advised Fund (DAF) Strategies

### What It Is
A charitable giving account that provides immediate tax deduction while allowing you to distribute grants to charities over time.

### Tax Optimization Plays

**Play 1: Donate Appreciated Stock (Not Cash)**
- Donate stock with large unrealized gains directly to DAF
- Deduction = full FMV of the stock
- Pay ZERO capital gains tax on the appreciation
- Better than selling stock, paying tax, then donating cash

**Play 2: Bunching Strategy**
- Donate 2-3 years of charitable giving into a DAF in one year
- Year 1: Itemize deductions (large DAF contribution pushes you over standard deduction)
- Year 2-3: Take standard deduction
- Net result: capture deductions that would otherwise be "wasted" below standard deduction threshold

**Play 3: Offset RSU Mega-Vest**
- In a year with a large RSU vest (high income), donate appreciated stock to DAF
- The charitable deduction offsets the RSU income
- You avoid capital gains on the donated stock
- Double benefit in a single transaction

### Limits
- Cash donations: deductible up to 60% of AGI
- Stock donations: deductible up to 30% of AGI
- Excess carries forward 5 years

## 4. Mega Backdoor Roth

### What It Is
Contribute after-tax dollars to your 401(k) above the normal limit, then immediately convert to Roth. Allows up to $46,000+ in additional Roth contributions per year.

### 2025 Math
- Employee 401k deferral limit: $23,500 ($31,000 if 50+)
- Total 415(c) limit: $70,000 ($77,500 if 50+)
- Employer match: say $10,000
- Available for after-tax: $70,000 - $23,500 - $10,000 = $36,500 of after-tax contributions
- Convert $36,500 to Roth immediately = Mega Backdoor Roth

### Requirements
- 401(k) plan MUST allow after-tax contributions (not all do)
- Plan MUST allow in-plan Roth conversion OR in-service withdrawal to Roth IRA
- Check with HR or plan administrator

### Companies Known to Support This
Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, Apple, NVIDIA, Salesforce, many large tech companies.
Also available through Solo 401(k) for self-employed individuals.

### Action Steps
1. Check if your employer's 401(k) allows after-tax contributions
2. Check if plan allows in-plan Roth conversion or in-service rollover
3. Calculate your available after-tax contribution space
4. Set up automatic after-tax contributions + automatic Roth conversion
5. Convert IMMEDIATELY after contribution to minimize taxable earnings

## 5. Tax-Gain Harvesting (Counterintuitive but Powerful)

### What It Is
Intentionally realizing capital gains in a year when your tax rate on those gains is 0% or very low.

### When It Works
- Total taxable income (including gains) below the 0% LTCG threshold
- Situations: early retirement, sabbatical, between jobs, low-income year
- Thresholds (2025): $48,350 single / $96,700 MFJ

### How It Works
1. Calculate how much LTCG you can realize at 0% rate
2. Sell appreciated assets to realize that amount of gain
3. Immediately repurchase (no wash sale rule for GAINS — only applies to losses)
4. Your cost basis is now reset higher → less tax when you eventually sell for real

### Example
Sabbatical year, taxable income = $30,000.
Headroom to 0% LTCG threshold: $48,350 - $30,000 = $18,350
Sell stock with $18,350 of long-term gains. Pay $0 tax.
Immediately rebuy. New cost basis is $18,350 higher.
If you later sell in a year when you're in the 15% bracket: save $18,350 × 15% = $2,753.

## 6. Net Unrealized Appreciation (NUA)

### What It Is
Special tax treatment for employer stock held in a 401(k). The appreciation (NUA) is taxed at LTCG rates instead of ordinary income when distributed in-kind.

### Who Benefits
- Employees with large amounts of company stock in their 401(k)
- Especially valuable when cost basis is low (early employees)
- Must be triggered by a "qualifying event": separation from service, age 59½, disability, death

### How It Works
1. Distribute the company stock in-kind from the 401(k) to a taxable brokerage account (NOT rolled to IRA)
2. Pay ordinary income tax on the original cost basis only
3. The NUA (growth from cost basis to current value) is taxed at LTCG rate when sold
4. Any appreciation AFTER distribution is also capital gain

### Example
Employee has $500,000 of company stock in 401(k). Cost basis: $50,000.
Without NUA: roll to IRA, all $500,000 taxed at ordinary rates when withdrawn (up to 37%)
With NUA: pay ordinary income on $50,000 basis, pay LTCG (15-20%) on $450,000 NUA
Tax savings: $450,000 × (37% - 20%) = $76,500

### Warning
- Must distribute ENTIRE 401(k) balance in the same tax year (can roll non-stock assets to IRA)
- Cannot selectively NUA just the stock — it's all-or-nothing for the stock portion
- Requires careful coordination with tax advisor

## 7. HSA Maximization Strategy

### What It Is
Use HSA as a stealth Roth IRA — the most tax-advantaged account available.

### Triple Tax Benefit
1. Contributions are tax-deductible (reduce current income)
2. Growth is tax-free
3. Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free

### The Power Move: Invest HSA, Pay Medical Out-of-Pocket
- Contribute maximum ($4,300 individual / $8,550 family in 2025)
- Invest in growth assets (same as you'd invest Roth IRA)
- Pay all medical expenses out-of-pocket (save receipts)
- Let HSA compound for decades
- In retirement: reimburse yourself for ALL accumulated medical expenses, tax-free
- No time limit on when you submit reimbursement — save receipts indefinitely

### Math
$8,550/year × 30 years at 8% return = ~$1,000,000 in tax-free medical reimbursement money.
Effective tax savings vs taxable investing: $200,000+

## 8. Backdoor Roth IRA (Standard)

### For High Earners Who Exceed Roth Income Limits
1. Contribute $7,000 to a Traditional IRA (non-deductible)
2. Immediately convert to Roth IRA
3. If you have NO other Traditional IRA balances: essentially $0 tax on conversion
4. If you HAVE other Traditional IRA balances: pro-rata rule applies — taxable portion calculated across ALL Traditional IRA balances

### Pro-Rata Rule Workaround
If you have pre-tax IRA balances that would trigger pro-rata:
- Roll pre-tax IRA balance INTO your employer 401(k) (reverse rollover)
- This removes pre-tax balance from the pro-rata calculation
- Now backdoor Roth conversion is clean

### Frequency
Do this every year. $7,000/year in Roth, tax-free growth forever.
Over 20 years at 8% return: ~$350,000 in tax-free wealth.
