# Asset Location Framework

Asset LOCATION (which account type) is different from asset ALLOCATION (which assets).
The same portfolio can have dramatically different after-tax returns based purely on
which assets sit in which account.

## The Core Principle

Place assets in the account type that minimizes their tax drag:
- **Tax-inefficient assets → tax-advantaged accounts** (Traditional IRA, 401k, Roth)
- **Tax-efficient assets → taxable accounts** (brokerage)
- **Highest growth potential → Roth IRA** (gains are never taxed)

## Asset Tax Efficiency Rankings

### Most Tax-Inefficient (put in IRA/401k first)
1. **Taxable bonds / bond funds** — interest taxed as ordinary income (up to 37%)
2. **REITs** — dividends taxed as ordinary income, no qualified dividend treatment
3. **Actively managed funds with high turnover** — frequent short-term capital gains distributions
4. **High-dividend stocks** — quarterly ordinary income events
5. **Commodity ETFs (non-equity)** — complex K-1 tax treatment

### Moderately Tax-Efficient
6. **Dividend growth stocks** — qualified dividends taxed at 15-20%, but still annual tax events
7. **Balanced funds** — mix of interest and dividends
8. **International stocks with foreign tax credit** — credit is lost in Roth

### Most Tax-Efficient (safe in taxable accounts)
9. **Total market index funds (VTI, FZROX)** — very low turnover, minimal distributions
10. **Growth stocks (no/low dividend)** — only taxed when you sell (you control timing)
11. **Tax-managed funds** — specifically designed to minimize distributions
12. **Individual stocks held long-term** — no tax until you decide to sell
13. **Municipal bonds** — federally tax-exempt interest
14. **Series I/EE Savings Bonds** — tax-deferred until redemption

## Special Considerations

### Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) in Taxable vs Roth
International stocks in taxable accounts qualify for the Foreign Tax Credit (reduces your US tax).
In a Roth IRA, foreign taxes are still withheld but you get NO credit — pure loss.
Rule: Keep international funds in taxable accounts to capture FTC.

### Roth IRA: Maximum Growth Assets
Since Roth gains are NEVER taxed, put your highest-expected-return assets here:
- Small-cap growth, AI/tech stocks, high-conviction positions
- The tax-free compounding on winners is the most valuable tax benefit available

### HSA: The Triple Tax Advantage
If eligible (high-deductible health plan):
1. Tax-deductible contribution
2. Tax-free growth
3. Tax-free withdrawal for medical expenses (even decades later)
Maximum 2025: $4,300 individual / $8,550 family (+$1,000 catch-up if 55+)
Optimal: invest HSA in growth assets, pay medical expenses out-of-pocket, let HSA compound

### 401k with Company Stock: NUA Strategy
If your 401k holds employer stock with large unrealized gain:
- Net Unrealized Appreciation (NUA) allows company stock to be distributed in-kind
- The NUA is taxed at long-term capital gains rate (15-20%) instead of ordinary income (up to 37%)
- Only the original cost basis is taxed as ordinary income at distribution
- Potentially massive savings for concentrated positions

### Tax-Loss Harvesting in Taxable Only
You can only harvest tax losses in taxable accounts.
Asset location should consider whether you want the ABILITY to harvest losses.
For volatile assets you might want to sell at a loss: keep in taxable.
For assets you'll hold forever: Roth IRA is optimal.

## Implementation Priority

When moving existing assets between account types (over time via new contributions):
1. Move bonds/REITs out of taxable → into IRA/401k
2. Move highest-growth stocks out of Traditional IRA → into Roth (via conversion)
3. Move international funds into taxable (for FTC)
4. Max HSA contributions and invest aggressively
5. Use tax-efficient index funds for remaining taxable assets

Note: Don't sell assets just to relocate them — the tax cost of selling may exceed the location benefit. Instead, redirect new contributions to the optimal account and let the portfolio naturally rebalance over time.
