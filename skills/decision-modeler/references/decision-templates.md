# Common Financial Decision Templates

## RENT vs. BUY

### Key Variables
1. Home price and expected appreciation (2-5%/yr historical)
2. Mortgage rate and term
3. Rent and expected rent inflation (3-5%/yr)
4. Investment return on money NOT used for down payment (7-10%/yr)
5. Marginal tax rate (affects mortgage interest deduction value)
6. Time horizon in the home
7. Maintenance costs (1-2% of home value/yr)
8. Property tax rate
9. Transaction costs (closing costs 2-5%, selling costs 5-6%)

### Breakeven Formula
The "price-to-rent ratio" gives a quick signal:
- Annual rent × 15-20 = fair home price (if below, buying may win)
- If ratio >20, renting + investing difference often wins

### Full Model
```
Buy total cost = Down payment opportunity cost + mortgage interest + property tax
  + maintenance + insurance + HOA + transaction costs - appreciation - tax savings

Rent total cost = cumulative rent - investment returns on saved capital
```

### Rule of Thumb
- Staying <5 years: rent usually wins (transaction costs eat gains)
- Staying 5-10 years: depends on local market
- Staying >10 years: buying usually wins (if price-to-rent is reasonable)

## ROTH CONVERSION (Convert Traditional IRA to Roth)

### Key Variables
1. Current marginal tax rate
2. Expected future tax rate (in retirement)
3. Amount to convert
4. Years until withdrawal
5. Source of tax payment (critical: pay from outside funds, not the conversion)
6. State tax rate (current and future)
7. Impact on ACA subsidies, Medicare premiums (IRMAA)

### When Conversion Wins
- Current rate < future rate (most common case for young high-earners)
- Low-income years (job loss, sabbatical, early retirement before SS)
- Tax rate arbitrage: convert to FILL a lower bracket
- Long time horizon (>10 years of tax-free growth)
- Avoid RMDs at 73+ (Roth has no RMDs)

### When Conversion Loses
- Current rate > future rate AND paying tax from conversion itself
- Conversion pushes you into a higher bracket
- Conversion triggers IRMAA surcharge (Medicare Part B/D)
- Short time horizon (<5 years)
- Need the money before 59.5 (5-year rule on conversions)

### Optimal Conversion Amount
Fill the current bracket but don't spill into the next:
```
Optimal conversion = Top of current bracket - Current taxable income
```
Example: In 22% bracket (tops at $100,525 for single 2025):
If taxable income is $75,000, optimal conversion = $25,525

## SELL vs. HOLD (Concentrated Stock Position)

### Key Variables
1. Current gain/loss and holding period (STCG vs LTCG)
2. Concentration risk (% of portfolio in this position)
3. Forward thesis (bullish, neutral, bearish)
4. Tax cost of selling now
5. Tax cost of selling later
6. Opportunity cost (what would you buy instead?)
7. Wash sale considerations
8. Account type (taxable vs. tax-advantaged)

### Framework: The "Tax-Adjusted Expected Value"
```
Hold EV = Current Value × (1 + Expected Return) - Future Tax on Gain
Sell EV = After-Tax Proceeds × (1 + Alternative Return)
```

If Sell EV > Hold EV, sell. But weight by confidence in expected returns.

### Concentration Risk Decision Matrix
| Portfolio % | Conviction | Action |
|-------------|-----------|--------|
| <5% | Any | Hold (not meaningful) |
| 5-15% | High | Hold with stop-loss |
| 5-15% | Medium | Trim to 10% |
| 5-15% | Low | Sell |
| 15-25% | High | Trim to 15% over 2-3 months |
| 15-25% | Medium/Low | Trim to 10% |
| >25% | Any | Trim to 15% (urgent) |

## STAY vs. SWITCH JOBS

### Financial Variables
1. Salary differential (gross and net)
2. Bonus structure and probability
3. Equity comp (RSU/ISO/NSO): vesting schedule, cliff, unvested value
4. 401k match differential
5. Health insurance cost differential
6. Other benefits (ESPP, HSA, commuter, tuition)
7. Relocation costs/benefits
8. State tax impact if moving

### Non-Financial Variables (assign subjective 1-10 scores)
- Career growth trajectory
- Work-life balance
- Manager/team quality
- Company stability
- Remote/hybrid flexibility
- Mission alignment

### Golden Handcuffs Calculator
```
Cost to leave = Unvested equity + Forfeited bonus + Benefit gap
Benefit of staying = Future vesting + Known promotion timeline
Benefit of leaving = Salary increase + New equity + Growth potential

Breakeven: When cumulative new comp exceeds cost-to-leave + staying benefits
```

## REFINANCE MORTGAGE

### Key Variables
1. Current rate vs. new rate
2. Remaining loan balance
3. Closing costs (typically 2-5% of loan)
4. Remaining term vs. new term
5. Monthly payment change
6. Break-even months

### Breakeven Formula
```
Breakeven months = Closing costs / Monthly savings
```
If you'll stay in the home longer than breakeven → refinance wins.

### Rules of Thumb
- Rate reduction of 0.75%+ usually worth exploring
- Rate reduction of 1%+ almost always worth it
- Don't restart a 30-year clock late in your mortgage (consider 15 or 20-year)
- Cash-out refi: only for high-ROI use (never for consumption)

## LUMP SUM vs. DCA (Investing a Windfall)

### Historical Data
- Lump sum beats DCA ~67% of the time (Vanguard research)
- DCA reduces maximum drawdown by ~30%
- DCA is a hedge against sequence risk, not a return optimizer

### Decision Matrix
| Situation | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| High risk tolerance, long horizon | Lump sum |
| Moderate risk tolerance | 3-month DCA |
| Low risk tolerance / anxious | 6-month DCA |
| Markets at ATH, high VIX | 3-6 month DCA |
| Markets down >20% | Lump sum (buy the dip) |
| Tax optimization needed | Spread across tax years |

### The Psychological Factor
If investing lump sum would cause anxiety that leads to panic selling during a drawdown, DCA is better DESPITE lower expected return. Behavioral risk > mathematical optimization.
